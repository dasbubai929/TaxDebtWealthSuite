Most Americans assume that because they paid taxes into the Social Security system for 40 years, the benefits they receive in retirement will be tax-free. This is mathematically incorrect.

Depending on your other sources of income, the IRS can tax up to 85% of your Social Security benefits. As part of our **[Retirement Planning Guide](/blog/retirement-planning-guide/)**, we explore exactly how this tax trap is triggered and how to structure your retirement accounts to avoid it.

## The "Combined Income" Formula

The IRS does not look at your total net worth to determine if your Social Security is taxable. They look at a specific metric called **Combined Income** (sometimes called Provisional Income). 

The formula is:
**Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) + Nontaxable Interest + 50% of your Social Security Benefits = Combined Income**

Note what is included in your AGI: W-2 wages, business income, capital gains, and—most dangerously—withdrawals from Traditional 401(k)s and IRAs. 

## The Taxation Thresholds

Once you calculate your Combined Income, the IRS applies the following thresholds (as of 2024):

**For Individuals:**
- Combined Income under $25,000: 0% of benefits are taxed.
- Combined Income between $25,000 and $34,000: Up to 50% of benefits are taxed.
- Combined Income over $34,000: Up to **85%** of benefits are taxed.

**For Married Couples Filing Jointly:**
- Combined Income under $32,000: 0% of benefits are taxed.
- Combined Income between $32,000 and $44,000: Up to 50% of benefits are taxed.
- Combined Income over $44,000: Up to **85%** of benefits are taxed.

Notice how incredibly low these thresholds are. A married couple withdrawing just $35,000 from a Traditional 401(k) and receiving $30,000 in Social Security will have a Combined Income of $50,000 ($35k + $15k). This throws them immediately into the 85% taxation bracket.

*Note: This does not mean you pay an 85% tax rate. It means 85% of the benefit is added to your taxable income and taxed at your ordinary marginal rate.*

## Strategy: The Roth IRA Shield

The most effective way to avoid Social Security taxation is to heavily utilize **Roth IRAs** and **Roth 401(k)s**.

Because Roth withdrawals are completely tax-free, they do not count toward your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI). 

If a married couple needs $60,000 to live on, and they withdraw $30,000 from a Roth IRA and receive $30,000 in Social Security, their AGI is $0. Their Combined Income is $15,000. Their Social Security is 100% tax-free.

Many retirees engage in **Roth Conversions** during their early 60s (before taking Social Security) specifically to drain their Traditional IRAs and move the money into Roth accounts, permanently shielding their future Social Security benefits from taxation.
