"Buy, Borrow, Die" has become the go-to phrase for how the ultra-wealthy allegedly avoid taxes. The internet is full of hot takes — half of them say it's a dirty loophole that should be illegal, the other half say it's a genius hack anyone can use. Both are wrong, and both miss the point.

*(Note: This guide is part of our comprehensive **[Tax Planning Strategies](/blog/tax-planning-strategies/)** series, exploring how high-net-worth individuals navigate liquidity without triggering taxable events.)*

The short answer: **yes, it is legal.** The core mechanics don't involve fraud, hidden income, or anything the IRS hasn't already accounted for in the tax code. But "legal" doesn't mean "simple," "risk-free," or "something you should do without running the numbers first." The strategy has real costs, real failure modes, and it requires a specific financial profile to work at all.

Here's what the IRS actually says — no hype, no hand-waving.

## What "Buy, Borrow, Die" Actually Means

The name sounds dramatic. The mechanics are straightforward — three steps:

1. **Buy** — You acquire assets that appreciate over time: stocks, index funds, real estate, a business stake. The longer you hold, the larger the unrealized capital gain grows.
2. **Borrow** — Instead of selling those assets to raise cash — which would trigger capital gains tax — you take out a loan against them, typically through a securities-backed line of credit (SBLOC). You get the cash. You keep the assets. No sale occurs.
3. **Die** — When you pass away, your heirs inherit the assets at a "stepped-up" cost basis. The decades of unrealized gains are wiped out for tax purposes. Your estate uses the inherited assets (or their sale proceeds) to repay the outstanding loan balance. You — or your estate — never pay capital gains tax on that appreciation.

This strategy wasn't invented in the shadows. It's what happens when you combine a few long-standing, well-documented tax rules that interact favorably for people with large, appreciated portfolios. The reason billionaires use it isn't that it's secret. It's that you need substantial assets with low volatility to make the borrowing component work without the whole thing blowing up.

To be clear: this isn't a strategy for someone with $50,000 in a brokerage account. SBLOCs typically require six- or seven-figure portfolios, and the loan-to-value ratios the strategy depends on only make sense when your assets are diversified enough to weather a market downturn without triggering a forced sale.

## Why Borrowing Against Stock Isn't a Taxable Event

The core principle here is one of the oldest in US tax law: **loan proceeds are not income.**

When you borrow money — from a bank, a brokerage, a friend — you receive cash, but you also take on an obligation to repay it. Those two things net out. You're not wealthier on a net basis, so there's nothing for the IRS to tax, per Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass, Tax Court.

This isn't some obscure loophole. It's the same reason your mortgage proceeds aren't taxed as income when you buy a house. Your brokerage lending you money against your stock portfolio works on exactly the same principle. You still own the stock. You haven't sold anything. No capital gains event has occurred.

### The constructive sale catch

The IRS doesn't dispute that loans aren't income. But they do watch for transactions that *look* like borrowing but *function* like a sale. That's where the constructive sale rules come in.

Under IRC §1259, if you enter into a transaction that eliminates substantially all of your risk of loss *and* opportunity for gain in an appreciated position, the IRS can treat it as a taxable sale — even if you never technically sold, per IRS.gov.

Here's what that means in practice:

- **A standard SBLOC** — you borrow 30–50% of your portfolio's value, you still own the shares, and you retain full exposure to gains and losses. This generally does **not** trigger constructive sale treatment. You're taking a real risk that the portfolio could decline while you still owe the loan.
- **Hedged positions** — if you use complex derivatives or forward contracts to eliminate your downside while simultaneously borrowing the full value of the position, the IRS can argue you've economically replicated a sale. That **is** a taxable event.

For most people using a standard securities-backed line of credit at a normal loan-to-value ratio: no taxable event, no income recognized. The key word is "standard."

## What Happens at Death — The Step-Up in Basis

This is the piece that makes the full Buy, Borrow, Die cycle work — and it's also the most politically contentious rule in the entire tax code.

When you die and your heirs inherit your appreciated assets, their cost basis is "stepped up" to the fair market value at the date of your death, per IRS.gov. Here's a concrete example:

> You bought stock for $50,000 thirty years ago. Today it's worth $800,000. Your heirs inherit it at a basis of $800,000. If they sell immediately, they owe **zero** capital gains tax on that $750,000 of appreciation. The gain simply disappears from the tax system.

### How the estate handles the loan at death

The mechanics work like this:

- The estate inherits the appreciated assets (now with a stepped-up basis).
- The estate sells whatever assets it needs to, or uses them as collateral to pay off the outstanding SBLOC balance.
- Because the basis was stepped up at death, any sale by the estate triggers **no** capital gains tax.
- The loan is repaid. The remaining assets pass to heirs.

This rule has been in the tax code for decades. It has survived multiple presidential administrations and repeated attempts at reform. It's the foundation of multi-generational wealth planning far beyond this one strategy. Without step-up in basis, your heirs would inherit your original cost basis — meaning they'd owe capital gains tax on every dollar of appreciation from the day you first bought the asset, per IRS.gov.

## Where People Get This Wrong — The Real Risks

Here's where the online hot takes fall apart. Buy, Borrow, Die is constantly framed as a "hack" that lets you live tax-free with no downside. That framing is dangerous because it ignores several real risks that can make the entire strategy unravel.

### Margin calls

An SBLOC is secured by your portfolio. If your portfolio drops sharply — the way it did in 2000, 2008, 2020, and 2022 — your lender can issue a **margin call**, requiring you to post additional collateral or repay part of the loan immediately.

If you can't do either, the lender sells your assets to cover the loan. That sale **is** a taxable event, and it happens at the worst possible time: when prices are down and you have no cash. The strategy fails exactly when markets are most chaotic.

### Floating interest rates

Most SBLOCs carry variable interest rates. In 2021, rates were near zero. By 2023, they were above 5–6%. If you're carrying a large loan balance at a floating rate and rates stay elevated for years, you're paying a meaningful ongoing cost that eats into — or completely eliminates — the tax benefit you were trying to capture.

### Concentration risk

The strategy works cleanly with a diversified index fund portfolio. It works much less cleanly if your wealth is concentrated in a single stock that can drop 70% while the rest of the market stays flat. Many people who *think* they're running Buy, Borrow, Die are actually running a far riskier version with a concentrated position.

### You still owe the loan

The loan doesn't disappear. It has to be repaid at some point, either by you during your lifetime or by your estate after you die. If your portfolio doesn't grow enough to cover the compounding loan balance, you may have less to pass on than you planned — and the whole rationale for the strategy breaks down.

**None of this makes the strategy illegal.** It makes it a real financial decision with real tradeoffs that deserve real analysis before you commit.
