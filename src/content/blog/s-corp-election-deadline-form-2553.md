The deadline to elect S-Corp status is **75 days from the date your business is formed or acquires assets** for new businesses, or **by March 15** of the tax year you want the election to take effect for existing businesses, per IRS.gov. Miss it, and you're waiting another full tax year. The clock is specific, the rules are rigid, and there's no "I forgot" extension.

*(Note: This guide is part of our comprehensive **[Small Business Tax Guide](/blog/small-business-tax-guide/)** series, exploring how founders structure entities to shield profits from payroll taxes.)*

If you've decided — or are close to deciding — that S-Corp taxation makes sense for your LLC or corporation, this is the article that tells you exactly when and how to file.

---

## The two deadline scenarios

### New business: 75 days from formation

If you've just formed your LLC or corporation and want it treated as an S-Corp from the start, you have **75 days from the date of formation** (or the date the entity acquires assets, whichever is later) to file Form 2553 with the IRS, per IRS.gov.

For example, if your LLC was formed on January 15, your deadline is approximately March 31. If it was formed on November 10, your deadline is approximately January 24 of the following year.

The key word is "approximately" — the IRS counts calendar days, not business days, and the exact deadline depends on how the 75-day window is calculated from your specific formation date, per IRS.gov.

If you're starting a new business and know you want S-Corp status, file Form 2553 as part of your formation process. Don't wait. There's no reason to let the clock run.

### Existing business: March 15

If your LLC or corporation has already been operating — say as a sole proprietorship taxed LLC or a C-Corp — and you want to elect S-Corp status, the deadline is **March 15** of the tax year you want the election to take effect, per IRS.gov.

For example, if you want S-Corp treatment for the 2026 tax year, you need to file Form 2553 by March 15, 2026. If you file on March 16, the election applies to the *next* tax year (2027), not the current one.

This is a hard deadline. Not "approximately March 15." **March 15.** The IRS does not grant extensions for this filing.

---

## What happens if you miss the deadline

Missing the March 15 deadline doesn't mean you can never elect S-Corp status. It means you're delayed by a full year. But there is a potential path to retroactive relief.

**Late election relief** may be available under Rev. Proc. 2013-30, per IRS.gov. This revenue procedure allows the IRS to accept a late Form 2553 if you can demonstrate **reasonable cause** for missing the deadline.

What qualifies as reasonable cause? The IRS considers factors like:

- The entity was newly formed and the person responsible for filing was unaware of the deadline
- A tax professional failed to file on time
- The entity relied on incorrect advice from an advisor

What does **not** qualify: simply forgetting, not getting around to it, or deciding late that S-Corp would have been beneficial, per IRS.gov.

The process for late-election relief involves filing Form 2553 with a statement explaining the reasonable cause, typically attached to your first tax return as an S-Corp, per IRS.gov. **Do not assume relief is guaranteed.** The IRS may accept or reject your request at their discretion.

The safest approach: don't miss the deadline.

## Step-by-step: what to put on Form 2553

Form 2553 (Election by a Small Business Corporation) is a two-page form. Here are the key sections:

**Part I — Entity information.** Legal name, EIN, address, formation date, state, and entity type (LLC or Corporation). Straightforward — match exactly what's on your formation documents.

**Part II — Election information.** The critical line is **Line 7**: the first tax year for the election. For a calendar-year business filing by March 15, write "Calendar Year 2026" (or whichever year applies), per IRS.gov. You'll also list the total number of shareholders and their details (name, SSN/TIN, shares owned, date of acquisition).

**Part IV — Selected entity information.** Confirm whether you have a fiscal tax year (most don't) and whether you have more than one class of stock (you shouldn't, if you're electing S-Corp).

**Signature block.** Must be signed by an officer (corporation) or member/manager (LLC). **All shareholders at the time of election must also sign** to consent, per IRS.gov. Missing signatures are a common cause of rejection.

File the completed form with the IRS Service Center designated for your entity's state, per IRS.gov.

---

## Common mistakes that get elections rejected

The IRS rejects or delays S-Corp elections for predictable, avoidable reasons:

- **Wrong tax year on Line 7.** If you write a fiscal year but haven't established one with the IRS, the election can be rejected. Most small businesses should write "Calendar Year [XXXX]."
- **Missing shareholder consent signatures.** Every shareholder must sign. One missing signature = deficient form, per IRS.gov.
- **Wrong EIN.** Using your personal SSN instead of the entity's EIN, or an EIN that doesn't match the entity name.
- **Filing too late.** If you miss the 75-day or March 15 deadline, the election won't take effect for the intended year.
- **Entity not eligible.** More than 100 shareholders, foreign shareholders, or multiple classes of stock disqualify you, per IRS.gov.
