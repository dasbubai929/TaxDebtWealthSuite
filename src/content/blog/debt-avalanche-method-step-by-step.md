The debt avalanche method is simple: pay minimums on every debt, then throw every extra dollar at the **highest-interest-rate debt first.** When that one's gone, roll the entire payment into the next-highest rate. Repeat until you're debt-free.

*(Note: This guide is part of our comprehensive **[How to Get Out of Debt](/blog/how-to-get-out-of-debt/)** series, exploring exactly how to restructure liabilities and map your escape timeline.)*

It's the mathematically optimal payoff strategy. Every dollar goes where it saves you the most in interest. Here's exactly how it works — step by step, with real numbers.

---

## Step-by-step instructions

**Step 1: List every debt you have.** Write down the balance, interest rate, and minimum monthly payment for each one. Don't include your mortgage — this method is for consumer debt like credit cards, personal loans, and auto loans.

**Step 2: Sort by interest rate, highest to lowest.** This is the key difference from the debt snowball method, which sorts by balance. The avalanche doesn't care how big the debt is — it cares how expensive it is.

**Step 3: Pay minimums on everything.** Every debt gets its minimum payment, no exceptions. This keeps you current and avoids late fees or credit damage.

**Step 4: Put all extra money toward the highest-rate debt.** Whatever you can afford above the minimums — $100, $500, $1,000 — goes entirely toward debt #1 on your list. Nothing else gets extra payments.

**Step 5: When the highest-rate debt is gone, roll its entire payment into the next one.** The payment you were making on debt #1 now attacks debt #2. Your total monthly debt payment doesn't change — it just shifts to the next target.

**Step 6: Repeat until all debts are gone.**

The power of the method is in Step 5. As each debt disappears, its payment rolls forward, making the next payoff faster. The snowball effect is real — it just starts with the most expensive debt instead of the smallest one.

## A full worked example

Let's say you have three debts:

| Debt | Balance | APR | Minimum Payment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Credit card | $6,000 | 22% | $120 |
| Personal loan | $4,000 | 10% | $100 |
| Store card | $2,500 | 7% | $50 |
| **Total** | **$12,500** | | **$270** |

You have **$500/month** available for debt payments. After paying minimums ($270), you have **$230 extra** to deploy.

### Month 1

All three debts get their minimums. The extra $230 goes to the credit card (22% — highest rate).

- Credit card payment: $120 + $230 = **$350**
- Personal loan: $100 (minimum only)
- Store card: $50 (minimum only)

### Month 6 (approximate)

After six months of $350/month on the credit card, the balance has dropped significantly. Let's say it's down to roughly $4,200. The personal loan is at ~$3,600 (minimum payments only barely covering interest at this point). The store card is at ~$2,200.

You keep going. The credit card is still the highest rate — it stays the priority.

### Month 14 (approximate)

The credit card hits **$0.** You've paid it off roughly 18 months faster than if you'd only paid the minimum.

Now you take the entire $350/month that was going to the credit card and add it to the personal loan payment:

- Personal loan payment: $100 + $350 = **$450**
- Store card: $50 (minimum only)

### Month 18 (approximate)

The personal loan hits **$0.** You roll that $450 into the store card:

- Store card payment: $50 + $450 = **$500**

### Month 19 (approximate)

The store card hits **$0.** All debts are paid off.

**Total time: approximately 19 months. Total interest paid: roughly $2,100** (this varies based on exact compounding and payment timing — run your own numbers for precision).

The key insight: by attacking the 22% card first, you minimized the time that expensive debt spent accumulating interest. Every month you delay paying off a high-rate debt costs you real money.

## How much more you actually save vs. debt snowball

The honest answer: **it depends on your specific rate spread and balances.** There is no universal "avalanche saves X%" number.

Here's why. The avalanche's advantage grows when:

- **The gap between your highest and lowest rates is large.** If your most expensive debt is at 22% and your cheapest is at 5%, the avalanche saves significantly more than if your rates are 12% vs. 9%.
- **Your highest-rate debt is also a large balance.** The longer a high-rate, high-balance debt sticks around, the more interest it compounds — and the more the avalanche saves by killing it early.
- **Your payoff timeline is long.** Over 3+ years, the compounding advantage of the avalanche adds up meaningfully. Over 6 months, the difference may be modest.

In practice, the avalanche typically saves anywhere from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars compared to the snowball, depending on these factors. For some people, the difference is significant. For others — especially if their rates are close together — it's surprisingly small.

The only way to know for your situation is to run both methods side by side on your actual debts. Which is exactly what our calculator does.

## When avalanche is harder to stick with

The avalanche has a real weakness, and it's not mathematical — it's psychological.

Because you're targeting the highest-rate debt first, that debt might also be your **largest balance.** If your 22% credit card is also your $15,000 monster, you could be making extra payments for 12+ months before it's eliminated. During that entire stretch, you're paying more than minimums and watching no debt actually disappear.

Compare that to the snowball, which might knock out a $500 store card in month two. That crossing-off-a-debt feeling is powerful. It reinforces the behavior. It makes you want to keep going.

The avalanche doesn't give you that feedback loop — at least not early on. For people who need visible progress to stay motivated, this is a real problem. Not because the method is flawed, but because humans aren't spreadsheets.

If you've tried the avalanche before and quit after a few months because it felt like nothing was happening, that's not a character flaw. It's a signal that you might need the snowball's psychological wins — or a hybrid approach where you knock out one small debt first, then switch to avalanche.
