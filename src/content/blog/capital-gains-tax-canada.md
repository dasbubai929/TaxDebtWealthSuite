When you sell an investment for a profit in Canada, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) wants a piece of the pie. However, unlike some countries that apply a flat rate to the entire profit, Canada’s system is based on an "inclusion rate." Understanding this mechanism is vital for any Canadian investor looking to maximize their after-tax returns.

## How Capital Gains Work in Canada

A capital gain is the profit you realize when you sell a capital property—such as stocks, mutual funds, real estate (other than your principal residence), or cryptocurrency—for more than its adjusted cost base (ACB). 

### The Inclusion Rate
The cornerstone of Canadian capital gains tax is the **inclusion rate**. Currently, the standard inclusion rate is 50%. 

This means you do not pay tax on your entire profit. Instead, only 50% of your capital gain is considered taxable. This taxable portion is then added to your other sources of income for the year (like your salary or business income) and taxed at your marginal federal and provincial tax rates.

For example:
- You buy shares for $10,000.
- You sell them for $30,000.
- Your total capital gain is $20,000.
- With a 50% inclusion rate, only $10,000 is added to your taxable income.

### Recent Changes to the Inclusion Rate
It is important to note that the Canadian government has recently introduced changes to the inclusion rate for high earners. For individuals, capital gains realized annually above a $250,000 threshold may be subject to a higher 66.67% (two-thirds) inclusion rate. Gains below $250,000 remain at the 50% rate. For corporations and trusts, the 66.67% inclusion rate applies to all capital gains.

## Capital Losses

If you sell a capital property for less than its adjusted cost base, you have a capital loss. In Canada, capital losses are highly restricted: they can *only* be used to offset capital gains; they cannot be used to reduce your ordinary income (like your salary).

If your capital losses exceed your capital gains in a given year, the net capital loss can be:
- Carried back up to 3 preceding tax years.
- Carried forward indefinitely to offset future capital gains.

## Strategies to Minimize Capital Gains Tax

### Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)
The ultimate tool for Canadian tax planning is the TFSA. Any investments held within a TFSA grow completely tax-free. When you sell an asset inside a TFSA, you owe zero capital gains tax, and withdrawals are not added to your taxable income.

### Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)
While RRSPs do not provide tax-free growth forever, they do provide tax-deferred growth. When you sell an asset inside an RRSP, you don't trigger immediate capital gains tax. However, it's important to understand that when you eventually withdraw funds from an RRSP or RRIF in retirement, the entire withdrawal is taxed as ordinary income, completely losing the favorable 50% capital gains inclusion rate.

### Spousal Loans
If one spouse is in a significantly higher tax bracket than the other, a prescribed rate spousal loan can be used to shift investment income and future capital gains to the lower-income spouse, reducing the overall family tax burden.
