When a beginner decides to start investing, their first instinct is usually to open a brokerage account and buy shares of companies they like—Apple, Tesla, or Amazon. 

This is known as **Active Investing** (picking individual stocks). 
The alternative is **Passive Investing** (buying an index fund that owns everything). 

As a core tenet of our **[Investing for Beginners](/blog/investing-for-beginners/)** guide, we advocate almost exclusively for passive investing. Here is the mathematical proof of why.

## The SPIVA Scorecard

Standard & Poor's produces a bi-annual report called the SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) Scorecard. It tracks the performance of highly-paid, Ivy League-educated professional mutual fund managers against the standard S&P 500 index. 

The results are devastating for the active management industry. 
Over a 15-year period, **nearly 90% of actively managed large-cap funds underperform the S&P 500.**

If the smartest financial minds on Wall Street—with access to Bloomberg terminals, insider networks, and armies of analysts—cannot consistently pick the right individual stocks to beat the market, what makes you think you can do it from your smartphone?

## The Problem with Stock Picking: Asymmetry

The reason active managers fail is due to the fundamental asymmetry of the stock market. 

Historically, the overall US stock market goes up. But that growth is not distributed evenly. Studies have shown that over the course of decades, roughly **4% of all publicly traded companies account for 100% of the net wealth generated by the stock market.** The other 96% of companies collectively match the return of treasury bills.

If you are picking individual stocks, you are trying to find the 4%. If you guess wrong and pick a company in the 96%, you lose to inflation. 

## The Index Fund Solution

An index fund solves the asymmetry problem by refusing to play the guessing game. 

When you buy a Total Stock Market Index Fund (like VTI) or an S&P 500 Index Fund (like VOO), you are buying a tiny sliver of *every* company. 
- You will own the 96% of mediocre companies.
- But crucially, you are mathematically guaranteed to own the 4% of hyper-growth companies. 

Those few hyper-growth companies will pull the average of your entire portfolio upward, delivering historical annualized returns of roughly 7-10%. 

## The Fee Drag

The final nail in the coffin for active investing is fees. 
A standard actively managed mutual fund charges an "Expense Ratio" of around 1.00% per year. A broad-market index fund charges around 0.03% per year. 

Over a 30-year investing horizon, that 0.97% difference in fees will literally consume hundreds of thousands of dollars of your compound growth. By choosing low-cost index funds, you keep your capital exactly where it belongs: in your own portfolio.
