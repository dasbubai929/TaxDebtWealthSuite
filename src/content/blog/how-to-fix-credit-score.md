A bad credit score is an invisible tax on your entire life. It forces you to pay double-digit interest rates on car loans, denies you the ability to buy a home, and subjects you to massive security deposits for basic utilities.

Fortunately, your credit score is not a moral judgment. It is a strict mathematical algorithm designed by FICO. As the final lesson in our **[Personal Finance Guide](/blog/personal-finance-guide/)**, we will break down exactly how this algorithm works, allowing you to manipulate it to repair your score as quickly as possible.

## The FICO Score Breakdown

To fix your score, you must understand the five factors that comprise it, and how heavily they are weighted:

1. **Payment History (35%):** Do you pay your bills on time?
2. **Credit Utilization (30%):** How much of your available credit are you currently using?
3. **Length of Credit History (15%):** How long have your accounts been open?
4. **New Credit (10%):** Are you aggressively applying for new loans?
5. **Credit Mix (10%):** Do you have a healthy mix of revolving debt (credit cards) and installment debt (car loans)?

## The Slow Fix: Payment History (35%)

Because payment history makes up the largest chunk of your score, a single 30-day late payment can drop your score by 100 points instantly. 

Unfortunately, there is no "fast hack" for a late payment. It stays on your credit report for seven years. The only solution is to immediately set up auto-pay for the minimum balance on every single account you own. Over time, as you stack months of on-time payments, the algorithm cares less and less about the late payment from three years ago.

*If you had a late payment due to a genuine emergency or administrative error, you can write a "Goodwill Letter" to the creditor begging them to remove it. It rarely works, but it costs nothing to try.*

## The Fast Hack: Credit Utilization (30%)

While payment history takes years to fix, **Credit Utilization** can be fixed in 30 days. It makes up 30% of your score, and it has no memory.

Utilization is calculated by dividing your total credit card balances by your total credit limits. 
If you have a credit card with a $10,000 limit, and your balance is $9,000, your utilization is 90%. The algorithm views you as a massive risk, and your score will plummet.

The optimal utilization ratio is **under 10%**. 

There are two ways to fix this instantly:
1. **Pay down the balance:** Aggressively dump cash onto the card. The moment the creditor reports the lower balance to the bureaus (usually once a month), your score will instantly jump. (Use our **[Credit Card Payoff Calculator](/credit-card-payoff-calculator/)** to optimize this process).
2. **Increase your limit:** Call your credit card company and ask for a Credit Limit Increase. If they raise your limit from $10,000 to $20,000, your $9,000 balance is suddenly only a 45% utilization. Your score will jump, even though you didn't pay a dime.

## Never Close Old Accounts

When people finally pay off a credit card they've been struggling with, their first instinct is to cancel the card out of spite. 

**Do not do this.** 

Closing an old account hurts you in two ways:
1. It reduces your Total Credit Limit, which instantly spikes your Credit Utilization ratio.
2. It eventually reduces your Length of Credit History (the 15% category).

Once the card is paid off, put a small recurring subscription (like Netflix) on it, and set it to auto-pay in full every month. Put the physical card in a drawer. You will slowly build perfect payment history while maintaining your credit limit.
