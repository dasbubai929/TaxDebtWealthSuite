## 1. How the Social Security Benefit Formula Works

Social Security is designed to replace a portion of your pre-retirement earnings. The Social Security Administration (SSA) uses a multi-step formula to convert your lifetime earnings record into your **Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)**—the monthly benefit you receive if you claim at your Full Retirement Age (FRA).

### Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)
First, the SSA adjusts your historical earnings for inflation up to age 60 using national average wage indexing. It then selects your **highest 35 years of indexed earnings**. If you worked fewer than 35 years, zero-earning years are factored into the average, which significantly lowers your baseline benefit.

The sum of these 35 highest years is divided by 420 months (35 years × 12 months) to determine your **Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)**.

### The 2026 Bend Point Formula
Once your AIME is established, the SSA applies a progressive three-tiered formula using statutory "bend points." For 2026, the bend point formula calculates your PIA as follows:
- **90%** of the first **$1,174** of your AIME, plus
- **32%** of your AIME between **$1,174 and $7,078**, plus
- **15%** of your AIME exceeding **$7,078**.

Because lower income brackets receive a 90% replacement factor while higher brackets receive 32% and 15%, Social Security is intentionally progressive, providing a higher income replacement percentage to lower-wage earners.

```
Example Calculation (AIME = $8,000):
• Tier 1 (90% of $1,174)               = $1,056.60
• Tier 2 (32% of $5,904 [$7,078 - $1,174]) = $1,889.28
• Tier 3 (15% of $922 [$8,000 - $7,078])  = $138.30
--------------------------------------------------
Primary Insurance Amount (PIA)         = $3,084.18 per month
```

## 2. Claiming Age Strategies: Age 62 vs. 67 vs. 70

Your Full Retirement Age (FRA) depends on your birth year. For everyone born in 1960 or later, the FRA is **67**. While you can claim Social Security as early as age 62 or delay as late as age 70, your claiming age applies a permanent percentage adjustment to your monthly PIA.

| Claiming Age | Percent of PIA | Permanent Adjustment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age 62** | **70.0%** | 30.0% Permanent Reduction |
| **Age 63** | **75.0%** | 25.0% Permanent Reduction |
| **Age 64** | **80.0%** | 20.0% Permanent Reduction |
| **Age 65** | **86.7%** | 13.3% Permanent Reduction |
| **Age 66** | **93.3%** | 6.7% Permanent Reduction |
| **Age 67 (FRA)** | **100.0%** | Full Baseline PIA |
| **Age 68** | **108.0%** | +8.0% Delayed Credit |
| **Age 69** | **116.0%** | +16.0% Delayed Credit |
| **Age 70** | **124.0%** | +24.0% Maximum Credit |

### The Impact of Early Claiming (Age 62)
Claiming at age 62 provides income 5 years earlier than FRA, but permanently reduces your check by 30%. For instance, a $3,000 PIA drops to $2,100 per month. Additionally, if you continue working while claiming early, the SSA applies the **Earnings Test**: $1 in benefits is withheld for every $2 earned above the annual exempt limit ($23,400 in 2026).

### The Power of Delaying (Age 70)
For every year you delay claiming past age 67, your benefit grows by an inflation-protected **8% simple interest per year** (Delayed Retirement Credits). Waiting until age 70 turns a $3,000 PIA into $3,720 per month—a 77% increase compared to claiming at age 62.

## 3. Taxation of Social Security Benefits

Many retirees are surprised to learn that Social Security benefits can be subject to federal income tax. Taxation is based on your **Combined Income**, defined as:

$$\text{Combined Income} = \text{Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)} + \text{Nontaxable Interest} + \left( \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Social Security Benefit} \right)$$

### Federal Taxation Thresholds
- **Single Filers:**
  - Below $25,000: **0%** of benefits taxed.
  - $25,000 to $34,000: Up to **50%** of benefits subject to income tax.
  - Over $34,000: Up to **85%** of benefits subject to income tax.
- **Married Filing Jointly:**
  - Below $32,000: **0%** of benefits taxed.
  - $32,000 to $44,000: Up to **50%** of benefits subject to income tax.
  - Over $44,000: Up to **85%** of benefits subject to income tax.

## 4. Integrating Social Security with Your Overall Retirement Portfolio

Social Security should not be viewed in isolation. It acts as an inflation-indexed, government-backed annuity that forms the floor of your retirement cash flow. To optimize your overall tax burden, integrate your Social Security timing with your tax-deferred accounts.

- **Roth Conversions in the Gap Years:** If you retire at age 62 but delay Social Security until age 70, you create an 8-year "gap period" of low taxable income. Use this window to execute systematic Roth conversions, reducing future Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs).
- **RMD Management:** Once you reach age 73 (or 75), mandatory withdrawals from traditional IRAs can push your combined income into the 85% Social Security taxation bracket. Modeling your RMDs alongside Social Security prevents unexpected tax spikes.
