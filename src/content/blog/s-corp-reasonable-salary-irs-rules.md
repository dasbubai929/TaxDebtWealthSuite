If you own an S-Corp, you have a powerful lever: you can pay yourself via salary (subject to payroll tax) or via shareholder distributions (not subject to payroll tax). The less salary you take, the less you pay in Social Security and Medicare taxes. The incentive to lowball is obvious — and the IRS knows it.

*(Note: This guide is part of our comprehensive **[Small Business Tax Guide](/blog/small-business-tax-guide/)** series, exploring how founders structure entities to shield profits from payroll taxes.)*

That's why the tax code requires S-Corp owner-employees to pay themselves a **reasonable compensation** for the services they provide. Not an arbitrary number. Not whatever you feel like. A defensible amount that reflects what someone in your role, in your industry, would earn as an employee.

Get this wrong, and the IRS can reclassify your distributions as wages — meaning you owe back payroll taxes, penalties, and interest. Get it right, and you're within your rights to take the rest as distributions and save thousands in self-employment tax every year.

Here's how to figure out where that line is.

---

## What "reasonable compensation" actually means

There is **no fixed percentage, no magic number, and no official IRS formula** for what counts as reasonable salary. This is one of the most widely misunderstood areas in small business tax, per IRS.gov.

You'll hear people online say things like "pay yourself 60% of your profit as salary" or "keep distributions at 50%." These are rules of thumb that have no basis in the tax code. The IRS has never endorsed a specific salary-to-distribution ratio. What they've said — through court rulings, audit guidance, and IRS publications — is that reasonable compensation is based on a set of factual factors unique to each business.

That means there's no shortcut. But there *is* a framework.

---

## The factors the IRS and courts actually look at

When the IRS evaluates whether an S-Corp owner's salary is reasonable, they look at the totality of circumstances. The key factors — drawn from court decisions and IRS audit guidance — include (per Watson v. Commissioner, Tax Court):

- **Training and experience** — What education, certifications, or years of experience does the role require? A CPA running a tax firm has different compensation expectations than a first-time consultant.
- **Duties and responsibilities** — What does the owner actually do day-to-day? Are they the sole operator, or do they manage a team? Are they performing the core revenue-generating work?
- **Time devoted to the business** — Is this a full-time commitment or a side project? An owner working 50 hours a week has a stronger case for higher salary than one working 15.
- **Comparable salaries** — What would a non-owner employee performing the same role earn in the same industry and geographic area? This is often the most persuasive factor in an audit.
- **Company compensation history** — Has the owner always taken a low salary, or has it varied over time? A sudden drop after electing S-Corp status raises red flags.
- **Profitability and distribution history** — If the company is highly profitable and the owner is taking large distributions alongside a tiny salary, the IRS will notice.

No single factor is determinative. But taken together, they paint a picture of what the owner *should* be earning — and anything significantly below that picture is a target.

## A worked example: too low vs. defensible

Let's make this concrete. Say you own a marketing consulting S-Corp that nets $200,000 in profit. You work full-time, have 12 years of experience, and perform all client-facing work yourself.

**Scenario A: $20,000 salary, $180,000 in distributions**

The IRS looks at this and sees a full-time consultant with a decade of experience taking a salary that's below the poverty line for a dual-income household. A comparable marketing consultant in your area earns $85,000–$120,000 as an employee. Your $20,000 salary doesn't survive scrutiny.

**Scenario B: $90,000 salary, $110,000 in distributions**

This salary falls within the range of comparable compensation for your role, industry, and location. It accounts for your full-time commitment and experience level. The remaining $110,000 in distributions is defensible — it reflects profit above and beyond what your labor alone would command on the open market.

Both scenarios save you payroll tax compared to taking all $200,000 as salary. But Scenario A invites an audit. Scenario B survives one.

The goal isn't to minimize salary. It's to set a number you can **defend with a straight face** if an auditor asks you to justify it.

## What happens if the IRS reclassifies your distributions

If the IRS determines your salary is unreasonably low, they can reclassify some or all of your distributions as wages. The consequences stack up quickly, per IRS.gov:

- **Back payroll taxes** — You'll owe the employee and employer portions of Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%) on the reclassified amount. That's up to 15.3% in back taxes.
- **Failure-to-pay penalties** — The IRS can assess penalties for underpayment of payroll taxes, typically 0.5%–25% of the unpaid amount depending on how long the underpayment has existed, per IRS.gov.
- **Accuracy-related penalties** — If the IRS determines the underpayment was due to negligence or disregard of rules, an additional 20% penalty may apply, per IRS.gov.
- **Interest** — Accrues from the original due date of the unpaid taxes.

In a worst-case scenario, an S-Corp owner who aggressively lowballed their salary for years can face a six-figure tax bill. The savings from the lower salary were real — but the penalty for getting caught wipes them out and then some.
