Public retirement income in Canada is built on two primary pillars administered by Service Canada: the **Canada Pension Plan (CPP)** and **Old Age Security (OAS)**. Together, CPP and OAS provide a predictable income baseline for Canadian retirees. However, because CPP is an earnings-based contribution scheme while OAS is a residency-based entitlement, optimizing your total payout requires understanding how claiming ages, residency years, and income clawbacks interact.

## 1. Understanding the Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

The **Canada Pension Plan (CPP)** (or QPP in Quebec) is a taxable, earnings-related monthly benefit designed to replace approximately 25% to 33% of your average pre-retirement employment earnings up to the Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings (YMPE).

### CPP Maximum and Average Monthly Rates
- **Maximum CPP Monthly Benefit (Age 65):** **C$1,364.60 per month** (C$16,375.20 per year).
- **Average CPP Monthly Payout:** Approximately **C$815.00 per month**.

To receive the maximum CPP payment at age 65, you must contribute the maximum allowable amount on your earnings to the CPP for at least **39 years** between ages 18 and 65. Service Canada automatically drops up to 17% of your lowest-earning years under the **General Drop-Out Provision**.

### CPP Claiming Age Adjustments (Age 60 to 70)
Standard CPP eligibility starts at age 65. You can claim CPP as early as age 60 or delay as late as age 70:
- **Early Claiming (Ages 60 to 64):** Your benefit is permanently reduced by **0.6% for each month** before age 65 (a **7.2% annual reduction**, reaching a **36% total reduction at age 60**).
- **Delayed Claiming (Ages 66 to 70):** Your benefit is permanently increased by **0.7% for each month** after age 65 (an **8.4% annual increase**, reaching a **42% total bonus at age 70**).

| Claiming Age | CPP Factor | Maximum Monthly CPP |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age 60** | **64.0%** (-36% reduction) | C$873.34 / mo |
| **Age 62** | **78.4%** (-21.6% reduction) | C$1,069.85 / mo |
| **Age 65 (Standard)** | **100.0%** (Full baseline) | **C$1,364.60 / mo** |
| **Age 67** | **116.8%** (+16.8% bonus) | C$1,593.85 / mo |
| **Age 70 (Max Delay)** | **142.0%** (+42.0% bonus) | **C$1,937.73 / mo** |

## 2. Understanding Old Age Security (OAS)

Unlike CPP, **Old Age Security (OAS)** is funded through general federal tax revenues and does not depend on your employment history. Eligibility is based solely on age and years of Canadian residence after turning 18.

### OAS Payout Rates & Residency Rules
- **Maximum Monthly OAS (Ages 65 to 74):** **C$713.34 per month** (C$8,560.08 per year).
- **Age 75+ Bonus:** At age 75, OAS automatically increases by 10% to **C$784.67 per month**.
- **Residency Requirements:** To get the full 100% OAS rate, you must have lived in Canada for at least **40 years** after age 18. If you have lived in Canada between 10 and 39 years, you receive a pro-rated amount (e.g., 20 years residence = 20/40 = 50% of full OAS).

### OAS Deferral Bonus
You cannot claim OAS before age 65. However, you can delay OAS past 65. OAS increases by **0.6% per month** delayed (a **7.2% annual bonus**), up to a maximum **36% increase at age 70** (C$970.14 per month).

## 3. The OAS Recovery Tax (Clawback)

Because OAS is funded by general taxes, high-income seniors are subject to the **OAS Recovery Tax (Clawback)**. If your total individual net world income exceeds the annual recovery threshold, you must repay part or all of your OAS benefit.

- **OAS Threshold Start (2026):** **C$90,997 per year**.
- **Clawback Rate:** You repay **15 cents of OAS for every dollar** of income above C$90,997.
- **Full OAS Cutoff:** OAS is reduced to C$0 once net income exceeds approximately **C$148,000**.

### OAS Clawback Strategy Example
If your individual income is C$100,000 (C$9,003 above the C$90,997 threshold), your annual OAS clawback is:

$$\text{Clawback} = 15\% \times C\$9,003 = C\$1,350.45 \text{ per year } (C\$112.54/\text{month})$$

To minimize the OAS clawback, strategies such as pension income splitting with a spouse, using TFSA withdrawals, or optimizing RRIF withdrawal schedules can help keep individual net income below the C$90,997 threshold.
