If you have spent your life accumulating highly appreciated assets—such as index funds, real estate, or a private business—you are sitting on a massive, unrealized tax liability. 

If you sell those assets to fund your retirement, you trigger capital gains tax. However, the U.S. tax code contains a provision that allows you to legally erase that tax liability upon your death. It is called the **Step-Up in Basis**, and it is the cornerstone of our **[Retirement Planning Guide](/blog/retirement-planning-guide/)** estate strategies.

## How Cost Basis Works

To understand the step-up, you must first understand "Cost Basis." 

Your basis is the original price you paid for an asset. 
- You buy a house in 1990 for $200,000. Your cost basis is $200,000.
- By 2026, the house is worth $1,200,000.
- You have a $1,000,000 unrealized capital gain. 

If you sell the house while you are alive (and assuming you exhaust your primary residence exemption), you will owe significant long-term capital gains tax on that $1,000,000 profit.

## The Step-Up Provision

Section 1014 of the Internal Revenue Code dictates that when a person dies and leaves an appreciating asset to an heir, the cost basis of that asset is "stepped up" to its Fair Market Value on the date of death.

Using the example above:
- You die in 2026. The house is worth $1,200,000.
- You leave the house to your daughter.
- Her new cost basis is not your original $200,000. Her cost basis is stepped up to $1,200,000.
- If she sells the house a week later for $1,200,000, her capital gain is $0. She owes absolutely nothing in capital gains tax.

The $1,000,000 gain that occurred during your lifetime is wiped clean by the IRS. 

## The "Buy, Borrow, Die" Strategy

Because the Step-Up in Basis is so powerful, the ultra-wealthy use a specific mathematical strategy to avoid selling assets during their lifetime: **Buy, Borrow, Die**.

Instead of selling stock to generate cash (triggering taxes), they take out a margin loan or SBLOC against the portfolio. They use the loan to fund their lifestyle. When they die, the portfolio receives a step-up in basis. The heirs sell a portion of the tax-free portfolio to pay off the loan balance, and keep the rest tax-free.
