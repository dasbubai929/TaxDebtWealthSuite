For decades, you've diligently contributed to your Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP), watching your investments grow tax-sheltered while enjoying the annual tax deductions. But the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) doesn't let that tax shelter last forever. Eventually, you have to transition from saving for retirement to drawing an income from those savings, and that means navigating the rules of Registered Retirement Income Funds (RRIFs) and their mandatory minimum withdrawals.

Understanding RRIF minimums is critical. If you withdraw too little, you face penalties. If you withdraw too much without a plan, you could pay far more in income tax than necessary and risk outliving your savings.

In this guide, we will break down the rules for RRSP-to-RRIF conversions, explain how the CRA calculates your minimum withdrawal, and provide strategies for managing your tax liability.

## The Age 71 Deadline

The most important rule in Canadian retirement planning is the age 71 deadline. By December 31 of the year you turn 71, you must close your RRSP. You cannot leave the money in the RRSP past this date.

You have three options for your RRSP funds when you reach this deadline:
1. **Withdraw the entire amount in cash:** This is almost always a terrible idea, as the entire sum is added to your taxable income for the year, likely pushing you into the highest marginal tax bracket.
2. **Purchase an annuity:** You can buy a guaranteed income for life from an insurance company.
3. **Convert to a Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF):** This is the most popular option. A RRIF functions much like an RRSP—you control the investments, and they continue to grow tax-sheltered—but with one major difference: you can no longer contribute to it, and you must make mandatory minimum withdrawals every year.

While age 71 is the deadline, you can convert your RRSP to a RRIF at any age before that if you want to start drawing an income earlier.

## How RRIF Minimum Withdrawals Work

Once you open a RRIF, you are legally required to withdraw a minimum amount every calendar year *following* the year the RRIF was established. The CRA sets this minimum amount as a percentage of the fair market value of your RRIF on January 1 of that year.

### The Calculation Factor
The percentage you must withdraw increases as you get older, reflecting the CRA's assumption of a shorter remaining life expectancy. 

- **If you are under 71:** The formula is `1 / (90 - your age)`. For example, at age 65, the minimum is `1 / (90 - 65) = 4.00%`.
- **If you are 71 or older:** The CRA prescribes a specific, increasing percentage factor. At age 71, it is 5.28%. At age 80, it is 6.82%. By age 95, it caps out at 20.00%.

*Example:* If your RRIF balance is $500,000 on January 1 and you are 72 years old, the CRA prescribed factor is 5.40%. Your minimum withdrawal for the year is `$500,000 x 0.054 = $27,000`.

### Using Your Younger Spouse’s Age
A powerful, yet often overlooked, rule is that you can choose to base your minimum RRIF withdrawals on the age of your younger spouse or common-law partner. Because the younger spouse has a lower prescribed percentage factor, this lowers your mandatory minimum withdrawal amount. 

This allows you to keep more money growing tax-sheltered inside the RRIF for longer. You must make this election when you first set up the RRIF; you cannot change it later.

## Tax Implications of RRIF Withdrawals

Every dollar you withdraw from your RRIF is fully taxable as ordinary income in the year you withdraw it. It is added to your other sources of income, such as the Canada Pension Plan (CPP), Old Age Security (OAS), and non-registered investment income.

### Withholding Tax
When you withdraw the *minimum* amount, financial institutions do not automatically withhold income tax at the source (though you can request them to do so). You are responsible for paying the tax when you file your return, which may require you to pay tax by quarterly installments.

If you withdraw *more* than the minimum amount, the financial institution is legally required to withhold tax on the excess amount at the following rates (in all provinces except Quebec):
- 10% on excess withdrawals up to $5,000
- 20% on excess withdrawals between $5,000 and $15,000
- 30% on excess withdrawals over $15,000

*Note:* This withholding tax is just an installment toward your final tax bill. Your actual tax liability is calculated when you file your return based on your total income for the year.

### The OAS Clawback
One of the biggest dangers of large RRIF withdrawals is the Old Age Security (OAS) recovery tax, commonly known as the "clawback." If your total net income exceeds a certain threshold (which changes annually, but is generally around $90,000), the government starts clawing back your OAS payments at a rate of 15 cents for every dollar above the threshold. Careful RRIF planning is essential to avoid unnecessarily triggering this clawback.

## Strategic RRIF Planning

Managing your RRIF isn't just about taking the minimum; it's about optimizing your lifetime tax bill. Here are several strategies to consider:

### 1. The Early Meltdown Strategy
Instead of waiting until age 71 to convert your RRSP, it can often make sense to convert a portion of it to a RRIF in your 60s, particularly if you retire early and are in a low tax bracket before starting CPP and OAS. By melting down the RRSP early, you pay tax at a lower rate and reduce the size of the RRIF at age 71, which consequently reduces your mandatory minimums later in life when your income might be higher.

While our [Roth Conversion Calculator](/roth-conversion-calculator/) is built for US tax codes, the concept of paying tax now at a lower rate rather than later at a higher rate is mathematically identical to an RRSP early meltdown.

### 2. Funneling to a TFSA
If you are forced to take a RRIF minimum withdrawal that you don't actually need for living expenses, don't just leave the after-tax proceeds in a regular taxable account. If you have available contribution room, deposit the excess into your Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA). Inside the TFSA, all future growth and income will be completely tax-free.

### 3. Pension Income Splitting
If you are 65 or older, income from a RRIF qualifies for pension income splitting. This allows you to allocate up to 50% of your RRIF withdrawal to your spouse or common-law partner for tax purposes. If your spouse is in a lower tax bracket, this can result in significant immediate tax savings and can help both partners stay below the OAS clawback threshold.

### 4. Planning for the Estate
When the last surviving spouse passes away, the entire remaining value of the RRIF is fully taxable as income on their final tax return. For a large RRIF, this almost guarantees that roughly half of the account value will be lost to taxes at the highest marginal rate. A proactive withdrawal strategy during your lifetime—even if it means withdrawing more than the minimum—can help smooth out your tax burden and leave a larger after-tax estate for your heirs.

## Conclusion

Transitioning from an RRSP to a RRIF is a major milestone in your financial life. The CRA rules surrounding minimum withdrawals are strict, and the tax implications are significant. By understanding how the factors work, leveraging your spouse's age, and strategically timing your withdrawals, you can minimize taxes and maximize the longevity of your retirement savings.
