Capital gains tax (CGT) in the United States is one of the most critical elements of wealth building and tax planning. Whether you're trading stocks, selling real estate, or realizing crypto gains, understanding how the IRS taxes your profits can mean the difference between thousands of dollars in your pocket versus the government's.

## What is a Capital Gain?

A capital gain occurs when you sell an asset for more than you paid for it. The amount you paid is known as your "cost basis." The difference between the sale price and your cost basis is your capital gain. Conversely, if you sell an asset for less than your cost basis, you have a capital loss.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains

The IRS heavily incentivizes long-term investment through the tax code. The most important factor in determining your tax rate is your holding period—how long you owned the asset before selling it.

### Short-Term Capital Gains

If you hold an asset for **one year or less** before selling it, your profit is considered a short-term capital gain. Short-term gains do not benefit from special tax rates. Instead, they are added to your ordinary income and taxed at your marginal income tax rate. For high earners in 2024, this could mean paying up to 37% in federal taxes on your gains, plus any applicable state taxes.

### Long-Term Capital Gains

If you hold an asset for **more than one year**, your profit is considered a long-term capital gain. Long-term gains are taxed at preferential rates: 0%, 15%, or 20%. The rate you fall into depends on your taxable income and filing status.

For 2024, the long-term capital gains tax brackets for Single filers are approximately:
- **0%:** Taxable income up to $47,025
- **15%:** Taxable income between $47,026 and $518,900
- **20%:** Taxable income over $518,900

For Married Filing Jointly:
- **0%:** Taxable income up to $94,050
- **15%:** Taxable income between $94,051 and $583,750
- **20%:** Taxable income over $583,750

> **Pro Tip:** If you are nearing the one-year mark on a highly appreciated asset, waiting a few extra days or weeks to sell can dramatically lower your tax bill.

## The Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

High-income earners must also be aware of the Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT). The NIIT is an additional 3.8% surtax that applies to the lesser of your net investment income or the amount by which your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds certain statutory thresholds:
- $200,000 for Single or Head of Household filers
- $250,000 for Married Filing Jointly

This means the highest effective federal rate on long-term capital gains can actually be 23.8% (20% top bracket + 3.8% NIIT).

## Strategies to Minimize Capital Gains Tax

### 1. Tax-Loss Harvesting
Tax-loss harvesting involves selling investments that have lost value to offset the gains you've realized in other investments. If your capital losses exceed your capital gains, you can use up to $3,000 of those losses to offset ordinary income. Any remaining losses can be carried forward to future years. (Check out our [Tax Loss Harvesting Calculator](/tax-loss-harvesting-calculator/) for more).

### 2. The "Buy, Borrow, Die" Strategy
For ultra-high-net-worth individuals, one way to completely avoid capital gains tax during their lifetime is to never sell the assets. Instead, they borrow against their appreciated portfolio using a Securities-Based Line of Credit (SBLOC). This provides liquidity without triggering a taxable event. When they pass away, the assets receive a "step-up in basis" for their heirs, effectively erasing the capital gains tax liability. Explore our [Buy, Borrow, Die Calculator](/buy-borrow-die-calculator/) to model this strategy.

### 3. Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts
Investing through tax-advantaged accounts like a 401(k), Traditional IRA, or Roth IRA allows your investments to grow tax-deferred or tax-free. If you execute trades within these accounts, you don't trigger capital gains tax events.
