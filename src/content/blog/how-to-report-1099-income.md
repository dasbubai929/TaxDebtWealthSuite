When you work a traditional W-2 job, your employer handles your taxes for you. They withhold money from every paycheck and send it to the IRS. At the end of the year, you file a simple return to settle any minor differences.

When you start a side hustle, you are entering the world of **1099 Income**. You are now a business owner in the eyes of the IRS, and you are entirely responsible for tracking, reporting, and paying your own taxes. 

As a core component of our **[Best Side Hustles](/blog/best-side-hustles/)** hub, here is the step-by-step guide to reporting your freelance income safely.

## Step 1: Track Your Gross Receipts (The 1099-NEC)

If a client pays you more than $600 in a calendar year for your services, they are legally required to send you a **Form 1099-NEC** (Nonemployee Compensation) by January 31st of the following year. They also send a copy directly to the IRS.

When the IRS receives that copy, their computers automatically expect to see that exact amount reported on your tax return. 

However, you must report *all* income you earned, even if a client paid you $500 and did not issue a 1099-NEC. Relying solely on the forms in the mail is a surefire way to underreport income and trigger penalties. Use bookkeeping software (or a rigorous spreadsheet) to track every dollar that hits your bank account.

## Step 2: Fill Out Schedule C

1099 income is not entered on the standard wage line of your tax return. It is entered on a dedicated form called **Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business)**.

Schedule C is divided into two main sections:
1. **Income:** This is where you tally up all your 1099-NEC forms and any other gross receipts.
2. **Expenses:** This is where you list all of the costs associated with running your side hustle.

## Step 3: Deduct Your Expenses

You are only taxed on your **Net Profit** (Income minus Expenses). Therefore, maximizing the Expenses section of Schedule C is your primary defense against a massive tax bill.

You can deduct any expense that is "ordinary and necessary" for your business. Common deductions include:
- Advertising and marketing costs.
- Software subscriptions (Adobe, AWS, web hosting).
- Legal and professional fees (including the cost of the CPA who prepares your Schedule C).
- Office supplies and equipment.

The total of these expenses is subtracted from your gross income to yield your Net Profit. 

## Step 4: Calculate Self-Employment Tax (Schedule SE)

Once your Net Profit is calculated on Schedule C, that number is transferred to **Schedule SE**. This form calculates your 15.3% Self-Employment Tax (Social Security and Medicare).

> You can preview exactly what Schedule SE will output by running your estimated Net Profit through our **[1099 Self-Employment Tax Calculator](/1099-self-employment-tax-calculator/)**.

After the self-employment tax is calculated, your Net Profit is then added to your other income (like your W-2 wages) on Form 1040 to calculate your final federal income tax liability.
