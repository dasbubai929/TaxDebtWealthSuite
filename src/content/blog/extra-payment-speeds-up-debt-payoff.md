If you're making minimum payments on a credit card or loan and wondering whether adding an extra $50 or $100 a month is worth the effort — the short answer is **yes, and the effect is bigger than you'd expect.** Payoff timelines aren't linear. Because of how amortization works, even small extra payments can cut months — sometimes years — off the end of your payoff period. Here's why, and how to see it with your own numbers.

## A worked example: $50, $100, and $200 extra per month

Let's say you have a credit card with a **$5,000 balance** at **18% APR**. Your minimum payment is **$100/month.** Here's what happens under three scenarios:

| Scenario | Monthly Payment | Months to Pay Off | Total Interest Paid | Interest Saved |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum only | $100 | ~97 months (8 years) | ~$4,700 | — |
| +$50/month | $150 | ~40 months (3.3 years) | ~$1,530 | ~$3,170 |
| +$100/month | $200 | ~29 months (2.4 years) | ~$1,060 | ~$3,640 |
| +$200/month | $300 | ~18 months (1.5 years) | ~$570 | ~$4,130 |

The math: adding $50/month cut the payoff time from **97 months to 40 months** — saving 57 months (nearly 5 years) and roughly $3,170 in interest. Adding $200/month cut it to 18 months and saved over $4,100 in interest.

These numbers are illustrative and will vary based on your actual balance, rate, and payment amount — but the pattern is consistent. Extra payments don't just shave a proportional slice off the timeline. They compress it dramatically.

## Why extra payments hit harder than they seem

The reason is amortization. When you make a minimum payment on a loan or credit card, most of that payment goes toward **interest first**, and only the remainder reduces your principal balance.

On a $5,000 balance at 18% APR with a $100 minimum:

- **Month 1:** ~$75 goes to interest, ~$25 reduces your balance
- **Month 12:** ~$68 goes to interest, ~$32 reduces your balance
- **Month 48:** ~$35 goes to interest, ~$65 reduces your balance

Early on, barely any of your payment is touching the principal. But when you make an **extra** payment, that entire amount goes directly to principal — it skips the interest-first ordering. Every extra dollar reduces the balance immediately, which means less interest accrues on the next month's calculation, which means more of your next regular payment goes to principal too. It's a compounding effect that accelerates over time.

This is why the effect is nonlinear: a $50 extra payment doesn't just "add $50/month to your payoff." It reduces your balance faster, which reduces your interest charges, which means more of every subsequent payment hits principal, which reduces your balance even faster. The tail end of your payoff period shrinks disproportionately.

## Why the effect is bigger late in a loan

Here's a counterintuitive fact: **an extra $100/month matters more in the later stages of a loan than in the early stages.**

Why? Because your balance is declining over time. When your balance is $5,000, an extra $100 represents 2% of the remaining balance. When your balance drops to $1,000, that same $100 represents 10% of the remaining balance. The relative impact of each extra dollar grows as the balance shrinks.

This is the opposite of how most people think about debt. They assume the "hard part" is at the beginning, when the balance is largest. But the mathematical leverage of extra payments increases as you go — which is one more reason to stay consistent even when progress feels slow early on.

It's also why the last few months of a payoff plan are where the magic happens. You're making the same payment, but each dollar is now doing far more work against a much smaller balance.

## Where extra payments make the most sense if you have multiple debts

If you're carrying more than one debt, the question becomes: which debt should get the extra payments?

The answer depends on your strategy, but the principle is the same: **extra payments are most powerful when directed at the debt where they save you the most per dollar.**

- **Avalanche method:** Extra payments go to your highest-interest-rate debt first. This maximizes interest savings because you're eliminating the most expensive balance as fast as possible.
- **Snowball method:** Extra payments go to your smallest balance first. This maximizes psychological wins — you eliminate debts faster, which builds momentum.

In both cases, the extra payment should go to **one debt at a time**, not spread across all debts. Spreading $100 across four debts at $25 each saves some interest, but concentrating the full $100 on one debt eliminates it faster — which frees up its minimum payment to roll into the next debt, creating a snowball effect.

The best use of extra payments: pick one debt, attack it with everything above the minimum, and repeat.

## One-time lump sums vs. recurring extra payments

A $5,000 tax refund applied to your debt is a one-time lump sum. An extra $100/month is a recurring payment. Both help — but they work differently.

**Lump sums** deliver a single, dramatic reduction in your balance. If your balance is $15,000 and you apply a $5,000 lump sum, your balance drops to $10,000 overnight — and every future payment is now calculated against a smaller balance. The interest savings compound from that point forward.

**Recurring extra payments** deliver a steady, compounding effect that builds over time. They don't have the immediate drama of a lump sum, but over 12–24 months, they can equal or exceed the impact of a one-time payment.

**The best strategy? Both.** If you receive a lump sum (tax refund, bonus, inheritance), apply it to your debt. And if you can sustain even a modest recurring extra payment, do that too. The combination is more powerful than either alone.

The reason is simple: the lump sum reduces your balance immediately, and the recurring extra payments keep reducing it faster going forward. You're attacking the debt from two directions at once.
