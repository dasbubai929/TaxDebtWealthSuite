The most terrifying question in personal finance is: *"How much money do I need to retire?"* 

For decades, the financial industry had no good answer. Then, in 1994, a financial planner named William Bengen published a paper in the *Journal of Financial Planning* that changed the industry forever. He established what is now known as the **4% Rule**, a cornerstone metric of our **[Retirement Planning Guide](/blog/retirement-planning-guide/)**.

## How the 4% Rule Works

The rule states that you can withdraw 4% of your total retirement portfolio in your first year of retirement. In every subsequent year, you adjust that dollar amount for inflation.

For example, if you retire with $1,000,000:
- **Year 1:** You withdraw 4% ($40,000). 
- **Year 2:** If inflation was 3%, you increase your withdrawal to $41,200.
- **Year 3:** If inflation was 2%, you increase your withdrawal to $42,024.

Bengen stress-tested this strategy against 50 years of historical stock and bond data. He found that even if a person retired at the absolute worst possible times in history (right before the Great Depression in 1929, or stagflation in 1973), a 4% withdrawal rate would survive for at least 30 years without the portfolio hitting zero.

## The Math Behind the Rule

The 4% rule relies on a portfolio composed of roughly 50% to 75% stocks (like the S&P 500) and the remainder in intermediate-term Treasury bonds. 

If the stock market historically returns an average of 10% before inflation (about 7% after inflation), withdrawing only 4% means you are leaving 3% of real growth in the portfolio. This buffer is designed specifically to protect you against **Sequence of Returns Risk**.

Sequence of Returns Risk is the danger that the stock market crashes heavily in the first few years of your retirement. If you are withdrawing money while the market is down, you are locking in losses and permanently destroying the portfolio's ability to compound when the market eventually recovers. The 4% rule is conservative enough to survive severe early-retirement bear markets.

## Is 4% Too Conservative?

In recent years, many financial analysts have criticized the 4% rule as being *too* conservative. 

In Bengen's data, the 4% rate was the absolute worst-case scenario. In the median scenario, a retiree using the 4% rule over 30 years did not just survive—they actually ended up with *more* money than they started with. 

Modern dynamic withdrawal strategies suggest starting at a higher rate (e.g., 5%), but remaining flexible. If the market drops 20% in a given year, you voluntarily reduce your withdrawal amount rather than blindly adjusting for inflation. Flexibility is the ultimate safeguard in retirement planning.
