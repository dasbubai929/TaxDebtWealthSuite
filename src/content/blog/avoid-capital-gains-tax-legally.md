Selling highly appreciated assets is one of the most expensive financial actions you can take. Depending on your income bracket and state of residence, liquidating your stock portfolio can trigger a capital gains tax bill exceeding 30%. 

For decades, ultra-high-net-worth individuals have utilized a mathematical alternative: **never sell.**

By leveraging your existing assets through Margin Loans or Securities-Based Lines of Credit (SBLOCs), you can access hundreds of thousands of dollars in liquidity entirely tax-free. This article serves as a deep dive supporting our core **[Tax Planning Strategies](/blog/tax-planning-strategies/)** hub, explaining exactly how portfolio leverage allows you to avoid capital gains tax legally.

## The Problem: The Cost of Liquidation

Imagine you hold a $1,000,000 stock portfolio with a cost basis of $200,000. You need $200,000 in cash to fund a real estate down payment. 

If you sell $200,000 worth of stock, you realize a proportional capital gain. Assuming a 20% long-term federal capital gains rate, plus a 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT), and state taxes, you could easily lose 30% of your gains to taxation. 

Furthermore, you trigger **Opportunity Cost**. The $200,000 you liquidated is no longer invested in the market. Over the next decade, assuming a 7% average annual return, that missing $200,000 would have compounded into nearly $400,000. 

## The Solution: Portfolio Leverage

Instead of selling, you can pledge your $1,000,000 portfolio as collateral for a loan. 

### What is an SBLOC?
A Securities-Based Line of Credit (SBLOC) functions similarly to a Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC), but it is backed by your investment portfolio rather than your house. (For a direct comparison, read our guide on **[SBLOC vs HELOC](/blog/sbloc-vs-heloc-accessing-cash/)**).

Because loan proceeds are not considered taxable income by the IRS, you can withdraw the $200,000 entirely tax-free.

### The Mathematical Advantage
While you do not pay taxes on the loan, you *do* pay interest to the broker. The viability of this strategy relies entirely on the spread between your borrowing rate and your expected portfolio return. 

If your broker charges 6% interest on the margin loan, but your portfolio appreciates at 8% annually, your investments are growing faster than your debt is accumulating. You effectively generate positive arbitrage while bypassing the IRS completely.

## The Ultimate Tax Shield: The Step-Up in Basis

If you carry these loans indefinitely, how is the debt ultimately settled? This brings us to the most powerful loophole in the U.S. tax code: the **Step-Up in Basis**.

When you pass away, your assets are transferred to your heirs. The IRS "steps up" the cost basis of those assets to their fair market value on the date of your death. 

In our earlier example, your original $200,000 cost basis is completely wiped out. If the portfolio is worth $3,000,000 when you die, your heirs inherit it with a cost basis of $3,000,000. They can immediately sell a small fraction of the portfolio tax-free, pay off the outstanding margin loan, and keep the vast majority of the wealth. 

This multi-generational framework is colloquially known as the "Buy, Borrow, Die" strategy. 

> [!WARNING]
> Leverage introduces significant risk. If your portfolio's value drops precipitously, your broker will issue a **Margin Call**, forcing you to deposit cash or liquidating your assets at the worst possible time.
