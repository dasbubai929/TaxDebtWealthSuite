If you need cash and don't want to sell assets — whether to avoid capital gains tax, maintain market exposure, or simply because you don't want to liquidate — you have two main options: borrow against your investment portfolio (SBLOC) or borrow against your home (HELOC). Both let you access liquidity without selling. Both have real risks. But they're fundamentally different products, and the right choice depends on what you actually own.

*(Note: This guide is part of our comprehensive **[Tax Planning Strategies](/blog/tax-planning-strategies/)** series, exploring how high-net-worth individuals navigate liquidity without triggering taxable events.)*

The core distinction is simple: **an SBLOC borrows against a securities portfolio; a HELOC borrows against home equity.** Different collateral, different risk profile, different mechanics. Here's how they compare.

---

## How each one works, side by side

### SBLOC (Securities-Backed Line of Credit)

An SBLOC is a revolving line of credit secured by your taxable investment portfolio — stocks, bonds, ETFs, mutual funds. You pledge holdings as collateral, and the lender extends a credit line based on their value.

- **Collateral:** Taxable brokerage accounts (typically not retirement accounts).
- **Typical rates:** Variable, often tied to SOFR. Rates typically range from 5–9% depending on portfolio size and lender (rates and terms vary by lender).
- **Typical LTV:** 50–95% of portfolio value, depending on composition and diversification (rates and terms vary by lender). Diversified index funds qualify for higher LTVs; concentrated single-stock positions get lower limits.
- **Setup speed:** Usually days from application to funding, with minimal paperwork.

### HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit)

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by the equity in your home. The lender places a lien on your property, and you draw against the credit line as needed.

- **Collateral:** Primary residence or investment property (investment property HELOCs have stricter terms).
- **Typical rates:** Variable, usually tied to prime. Rates commonly range from 7–10%, though strong credit may qualify for lower (rates and terms vary by lender).
- **Typical LTV:** Most lenders allow 70–85% combined loan-to-value (CLTV), including your existing mortgage balance (rates and terms vary by lender). A $500K home with a $200K mortgage might qualify for a $100K–$150K HELOC.
- **Setup speed:** Typically 3–6 weeks. Requires appraisal, title work, and more extensive documentation (rates and terms vary by lender).

---

## Speed and paperwork

This is where SBLOCs pull ahead for people who need cash quickly. The SBLOC process is largely automated — the lender evaluates your portfolio, sets a credit line, and funds the account. No appraisal, no title search, no closing attorney.

HELOCs involve the same closing process as a mortgage: application, credit check, appraisal, title search, closing documents, and a waiting period. The entire process typically takes 4–6 weeks.

If you need cash in two weeks for a time-sensitive opportunity, the SBLOC wins on speed. If you're planning ahead and have a longer timeline, the HELOC's slower process isn't a real disadvantage.

---

## The risk that matters most for each

This is the most important section of this comparison. Both products carry real risk — but the nature of that risk is fundamentally different.

### SBLOC: margin call risk

Your SBLOC is secured by your portfolio. If your portfolio drops sharply, your lender can issue a **margin call** — requiring you to post additional collateral or repay part of the loan immediately. If you can't, the lender force-sells your securities at prevailing market prices.

This means the SBLOC can force you to sell assets at the worst possible time — when markets are down and you have no cash. For someone running the "borrow instead of sell" strategy, this is the primary failure mode.

The good news: margin call risk is manageable if you borrow well below your maximum LTV and maintain a diversified portfolio.

### HELOC: losing your home

A HELOC is secured by your home. If you default, the lender can foreclose. This isn't theoretical — it's the mechanism by which thousands of homeowners lose their properties every year.

The HELOC doesn't have "margin calls" in the dramatic sense. But it has the same fundamental vulnerability: if you can't pay, you lose the collateral. And unlike a portfolio, a home is something most people can't afford to lose.

## A simple comparison

| | SBLOC | HELOC |
|---|---|---|
| **Collateral** | Taxable investment portfolio | Home equity |
| **Rate type** | Variable (SOFR-based) | Variable (prime-based) |
| **Typical rate range** | 5–9% (rates and terms vary by lender) | 7–10% (rates and terms vary by lender) |
| **Typical LTV/CLTV** | 50–95% of portfolio (rates and terms vary by lender) | 70–85% of home equity (rates and terms vary by lender) |
| **Primary risk** | Margin call / forced liquidation | Foreclosure / loss of home |
| **Setup speed** | Days | 3–6 weeks |
| **Tax treatment of interest** | Generally not deductible | May be deductible if used for home improvements, per IRS.gov |
| **Best for** | Investors with large, diversified portfolios who need fast liquidity | Homeowners with significant equity who need a lower-rate, longer-term credit line |

Neither option is universally better. An SBLOC makes more sense if you have a large, diversified portfolio and want fast access to cash without touching your home. A HELOC makes more sense if your equity is primarily in your home, you prefer a potentially lower rate, and you have a longer timeline.

Some people use both — an SBLOC for short-term liquidity needs and a HELOC as a backstop or for larger, planned expenses. The right structure depends on your asset mix, your risk tolerance, and how much liquidity you actually need.
