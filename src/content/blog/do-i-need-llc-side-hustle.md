The moment someone decides to start a side hustle, they immediately assume they need to "form an LLC" to make it real. 

As part of our **[Best Side Hustles](/blog/best-side-hustles/)** strategy guide, we want to dispel this myth. An LLC (Limited Liability Company) is not a magic wand that instantly makes you a legitimate business, nor is it a tax loophole. It is a specific legal tool with a specific purpose.

## The Tax Myth

The biggest misconception about LLCs is that they save you money on taxes. They do not. 

By default, the IRS considers a Single-Member LLC a "disregarded entity." This means the IRS literally ignores the existence of the LLC. All of the profit from the LLC flows directly onto your personal tax return (Schedule C), exactly as it would if you were just operating under your own name as a Sole Proprietor.

Whether you are a Sole Proprietor or a Single-Member LLC, you pay the exact same income tax and the exact same 15.3% Self-Employment Tax.

*Note: The only way an LLC saves you money on taxes is if you make an S-Corp election, which rarely makes mathematical sense until your side hustle is generating over $80,000 in net profit.*

## The Liability Reality

If an LLC doesn't save you taxes, what does it do? It provides a **corporate veil**.

If you operate as a Sole Proprietor and a client sues you, they are suing *you personally*. Your personal bank accounts, your house, and your car are all exposed to the lawsuit. 

If you operate as an LLC, the client is suing the *company*. Assuming you have maintained the corporate veil (by keeping separate bank accounts and not mixing personal and business funds), your personal assets are generally protected. Only the assets owned by the LLC are at risk.

## When to Form an LLC

You should form an LLC when your side hustle carries inherent liability risk:
- You are working in a physical space (e.g., personal training, landscaping) where someone could get injured.
- You are manufacturing or selling physical products that could cause harm.
- You are handling highly sensitive data or providing consulting that could result in massive financial losses for your client if you make a mistake.

If you are simply doing freelance writing, graphic design, or coding from your laptop, the liability risk is often incredibly low. In these cases, operating as a Sole Proprietor (and perhaps purchasing a cheap professional liability insurance policy) is often the smartest, most cost-effective way to launch.
