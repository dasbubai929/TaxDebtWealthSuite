The Australian superannuation system is designed to help you save for retirement during your working years and then provide a steady income stream when you finally hang up your boots. When you reach your "preservation age" and retire, or when you turn 65, you gain full access to your super. 

At this point, most Australians choose to convert their accumulation super account into a retirement income stream, most commonly an **Account-Based Pension** (also known as an allocated pension). In exchange for zero tax on investment earnings within the pension phase, the government requires you to withdraw a minimum amount from this account each financial year.

Understanding these minimum drawdown rules is crucial to ensure compliance, maintain your tax-free status, and effectively manage your retirement cash flow.

## What is an Account-Based Pension?

An Account-Based Pension is a regular income stream bought with money from your super fund. It is highly tax-effective. For most people aged 60 and over, the pension payments you receive are completely tax-free. Furthermore, the investment earnings generated by the assets supporting the pension are also tax-free (unlike in the accumulation phase, where earnings are taxed at up to 15%).

However, the government wants this money to be used for retirement income, not as a perpetual tax-free estate planning vehicle. Therefore, they mandate minimum annual withdrawals.

## How Minimum Drawdown Rates Work

The minimum amount you must withdraw is calculated as a percentage of your account balance. This percentage increases as you get older. 

The calculation is made on **July 1** of each financial year (or the day your pension commences, if it starts partway through the year). 

Here are the standard minimum percentage factors based on your age on July 1:
- **Under 65:** 4%
- **65 to 74:** 5%
- **75 to 79:** 6%
- **80 to 84:** 7%
- **85 to 89:** 9%
- **90 to 94:** 11%
- **95 and older:** 14%

*Example:* If you are 67 years old on July 1 and your Account-Based Pension balance is $600,000, your minimum drawdown percentage is 5%. Therefore, you must withdraw at least $30,000 during that financial year (`$600,000 x 0.05`). 

You can take this as a single lump sum or as regular payments (e.g., monthly, quarterly) throughout the year, as long as the total by June 30 meets the minimum. Find your exact requirement using our [Drawdown Calculator](/rmd-calculator/australia/).

### Pro-Rata Calculations for New Pensions
If you commence an Account-Based Pension partway through the financial year, your minimum withdrawal for that first year is calculated pro-rata based on the number of days remaining in the financial year. 

*Exception:* If you commence the pension on or after **June 1**, you are not required to make any minimum withdrawal for that financial year. The minimum requirements will kick in on the following July 1.

## What Happens if You Don't Meet the Minimum?

Failing to withdraw the required minimum amount by June 30 has severe consequences. 

If you fall short, the ATO considers that your pension has ceased for that income year. This means your account reverts to "accumulation phase" for tax purposes. The investment earnings on your account for that entire financial year will lose their tax-free exemption and will be taxed at the standard superannuation accumulation rate of 15%. 

The ATO does offer some leniency for small, unintentional underpayments. If the shortfall is less than 1/12th of the required minimum and you catch up as soon as practicable (usually within 28 days), the ATO may exercise discretion and allow the pension to continue without penalty. However, it is best not to rely on this.

## Is There a Maximum Drawdown?

For a standard Account-Based Pension, there is **no maximum limit** on withdrawals. You can withdraw 20%, 50%, or even 100% of your account balance in a single year if you choose. 

*Exception:* If you are using a Transition to Retirement (TTR) pension—which allows you to access your super while you are still working—there is a strict maximum withdrawal limit of **10%** of your account balance each financial year.

## Strategies for Managing Your Drawdowns

While the government forces you to take the money out of the super environment, they don't dictate what you have to do with it once it hits your bank account. Here are strategies to manage the cash flow effectively:

### 1. Reinvesting Excess Income
If the mandatory minimum drawdown is greater than what you actually need for your living expenses, don't just leave it sitting in a low-interest checking account. You can reinvest the excess funds into investments outside of super (such as a brokerage account or term deposits). 

While the earnings on these investments will now be subject to your personal marginal tax rate, keeping the money invested helps combat inflation. 

### 2. Spousal Contributions
If you withdraw more than you need, and you have a younger spouse who is still working or eligible to contribute to super, you can use your excess pension payments to make a non-concessional (after-tax) contribution into their super account. This moves the capital back into the tax-advantaged super environment and can help balance account sizes between spouses.

### 3. Timing Your Withdrawals
You can choose when to take your payments. Some retirees prefer a lump sum at the start of the year on July 1 to fund a holiday or large purchase. Others prefer monthly payments to mimic a salary. 

From an investment perspective, leaving the money in the pension account for as long as possible (i.e., taking a lump sum in June) allows the funds to generate tax-free earnings for almost the entire year. Conversely, if markets are volatile, regular monthly payments act as "dollar-cost averaging" in reverse, which can help smooth out sequence of returns risk.

### 4. Reviewing Your Asset Allocation
Because you are required to withdraw cash every year, you must ensure your pension account has sufficient liquidity. It is common practice to hold 1 to 3 years' worth of anticipated pension payments in cash or short-term fixed interest within the super fund. This prevents you from being forced to sell growth assets (like shares or property) during a market downturn just to meet your minimum drawdown requirement.

## Conclusion

The minimum drawdown rules for Account-Based Pensions are designed to ensure superannuation is used for its intended purpose: retirement income. By understanding the percentages, monitoring your age bands, and ensuring compliance by June 30 each year, you can enjoy the fantastic tax benefits that the Australian pension phase offers.
