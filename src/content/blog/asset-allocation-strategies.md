The most common mistake new investors make is assuming that "picking the right investment" is the hardest part of finance. 

As we established in our **[Investing for Beginners](/blog/investing-for-beginners/)** guide, picking the investment is incredibly easy: just buy broad-market index funds. 

The actual challenge of investing is **Asset Allocation**: determining exactly what percentage of your total net worth should be held in risky assets (stocks) versus safe assets (bonds and cash). 

## The Role of Stocks vs Bonds

To build a portfolio, you must understand the job of each asset class:
- **Stocks (Equities):** Their job is to grow your money faster than inflation. They provide high returns, but they are highly volatile. They can drop 30% in a single year.
- **Bonds (Fixed Income):** Their job is to protect your money. They provide lower, steady returns. When the stock market crashes, bonds generally hold their value (or even go up), acting as a shock absorber for your portfolio.

## The Math of Time Horizons

Your asset allocation should be dictated entirely by one variable: **When do you need to spend the money?**

If you are 30 years old and investing for a retirement that is 35 years away, a stock market crash tomorrow does not affect you mathematically. You don't need the money tomorrow. Therefore, your portfolio should be highly aggressive (e.g., 90% Stocks / 10% Bonds) to maximize compounding growth over the next three decades.

If you are 64 years old and retiring next year, a 30% stock market crash is catastrophic. If you are forced to sell stocks while they are down to pay for your groceries, you permanently destroy your wealth. Therefore, your portfolio must be much more conservative (e.g., 60% Stocks / 40% Bonds) to ensure you have a safe pool of bonds to draw from during a recession.

## Rule of Thumb: The 110 Rule

A common (though generalized) heuristic for determining your asset allocation is the "110 Rule." 
Take the number 110 and subtract your age. The result is the percentage of your portfolio that should be in stocks.

- **Age 30:** 110 - 30 = 80% Stocks / 20% Bonds.
- **Age 50:** 110 - 50 = 60% Stocks / 40% Bonds.
- **Age 70:** 110 - 70 = 40% Stocks / 60% Bonds.

## The Importance of Rebalancing

Because stocks grow faster than bonds, your portfolio will naturally drift out of alignment over time. 
If you start the year at 80/20, a massive stock market rally might push your portfolio to 90/10 by December. Your portfolio is now significantly riskier than you intended.

To fix this, you must **Rebalance** once a year. You sell off the 10% excess stock and use the cash to buy bonds, bringing the portfolio back to your 80/20 target. Mathematically, rebalancing forces you to execute the ultimate investing mandate: it automatically forces you to "sell high" (the stocks that went up) and "buy low" (the bonds that lagged).
