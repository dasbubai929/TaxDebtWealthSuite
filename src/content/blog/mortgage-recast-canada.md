Homeowners in Canada navigating 5-year fixed or variable-rate mortgage terms often seek ways to deploy lump-sum funds—such as annual employment bonuses, inheritance, or tax refunds—to lower their ongoing monthly financial commitments. While the term "mortgage recast" is an American tax term, Canadian financial institutions offer equivalent mechanisms under statutory **Prepayment Privileges** governed by the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC).

## 1. How Prepayment Privileges Work in Canada

In Canada, most closed fixed and variable-rate mortgages include **Annual Prepayment Privileges**. These allow you to pay down a portion of your principal balance faster without triggering an Early Redemption Penalty (Prepayment Charge).

### Typical Canadian Prepayment Privileges
- **Lump-Sum Prepayment Privilege:** Most Canadian lenders (such as RBC, TD, Scotiabank, BMO, and CIBC) allow you to pay off **10% to 20% of your original mortgage principal** in lump sums each calendar year.
- **Payment Increase Privilege:** Many lenders allow you to permanently or temporarily increase your regular monthly payment by up to 10%–20%.
- **Double-Up Payments:** Allows you to pay an extra principal payment on any scheduled payment date.

## 2. Requesting a Payment Recalculation (Recast) in Canada

When you make a large lump-sum principal prepayment under Canadian rules, lenders default to keeping your monthly payment the same while shortening your overall amortization schedule. However, if your primary goal is to **reduce your monthly cash flow commitment**, you must explicitly request a **Payment Recalculation (Recast)** from your mortgage lender.

- **Mid-Term Recalculation:** Some Canadian lenders permit a formal payment recalculation mid-term for a nominal administrative processing fee (typically C$100 to C$300).
- **Term Renewal Recalculation:** Every 3 to 5 years when your Canadian mortgage term expires, your balance is automatically re-amortized over the remaining term at current market rates, achieving a recast effect without extra fees.

---

## 3. Comparing Prepayment Penalty Charges

If you exceed your annual lump-sum allowance limit (e.g., paying off 25% when your contract limit is 15%), Canadian lenders enforce prepayment penalties:
- **Variable-Rate Mortgages:** Penalty is typically **3 months of interest** on the excess prepayment.
- **Fixed-Rate Mortgages:** Penalty is the greater of 3 months of interest or the **Interest Rate Differential (IRD)** calculation.
