The State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction has long been a lifeline for taxpayers living in high-tax states. But ever since the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) introduced the SALT cap deduction, high earners have faced a harsh reality: a strict IRS $10,000 cap on how much they can deduct. Understanding this limitation is crucial for your financial health, as it directly impacts your taxable income and overall tax bill.

In this guide, we'll break down how the $10,000 limit works, walk through real-world examples, and explore the best tax planning strategies to help you minimize its bite.

---

## What is the SALT Cap ($10,000 Limit)?

Before the TCJA, taxpayers who itemized their deductions could deduct the full amount of their state and local property, income, and sales taxes from their federal taxable income. For residents of states with high income and property taxes, this was a massive tax shield.

However, the current law imposes a hard ceiling. The **IRS $10,000 cap** means that no matter how much you pay in state and local taxes, you can only deduct up to $10,000 (or $5,000 if married filing separately) on your federal tax return. Anything you pay above this cap simply vanishes as a federal deduction, effectively raising your federal tax liability.

*(Note: While the controversial One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) introduced adjustments to this cap starting in 2025, the underlying mechanics of phaseouts and deduction limits remain a critical piece of the tax puzzle for high-net-worth individuals.)*

## Real-World Examples: The High-Tax State Squeeze

The SALT cap deduction disproportionately affects taxpayers in states like New York, California, and New Jersey, where property and income taxes are notoriously high. 

### Example 1: The New Jersey Homeowner
Let's say you live in New Jersey and pay $15,000 a year in property taxes alone. On top of that, you pay $10,000 in state income taxes. Before the cap, you could deduct the full $25,000. Now, you are capped at $10,000. You lose out on $15,000 worth of deductions, meaning you owe federal taxes on $15,000 of income that used to be sheltered.

### Example 2: The California Earner
A high earner in California might pay $30,000 in state income taxes and $8,000 in property taxes. Total SALT paid: $38,000. Because of the IRS $10,000 cap, $28,000 of those taxes cannot be deducted federally. 

In both scenarios, the taxpayers are effectively double-taxed on a significant portion of their income—once by the state, and again by the federal government.

## Tax Planning Strategies to Navigate the Cap

Just because the IRS $10,000 cap exists doesn't mean you are out of options. Smart **tax planning strategies** can help you reclaim some of that lost tax benefit.

### 1. Bunching Deductions
If your total itemized deductions normally hover right around the standard deduction threshold, consider "bunching" your deductions. This means pushing two years' worth of deductible expenses (like charitable contributions or prepaid mortgage interest) into a single tax year. You itemize in Year 1 to get a massive deduction, and then take the standard deduction in Year 2.

### 2. Strategic Charitable Contributions
Using a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) is a powerful way to execute the bunching strategy. By front-loading several years of charitable giving into a single year, you can easily exceed the standard deduction threshold, maximizing your tax savings while still pacing your actual grants to charities over time.

### 3. SALT Workaround Programs (PTET)
If you own a business (like an S-Corp or LLC), you may be able to bypass the cap entirely. Many states have enacted Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) workarounds. This allows the business to pay the state tax on your behalf and deduct it as a business expense at the federal level, bypassing the individual SALT cap deduction limit. Consult with your CPA to see if your state offers a PTET election.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### What is the SALT cap deduction limit?
The SALT cap limits the amount of state and local taxes (property, income, and sales taxes) you can deduct on your federal tax return to $10,000 per year ($5,000 if married filing separately).

### Will the IRS $10,000 cap expire?
The original $10,000 cap introduced by the TCJA was scheduled to expire at the end of 2025. However, recent legislation (like the OBBBA) has altered the timeline and cap limits for subsequent years. Always check the current tax year's rules or use our calculator for the most up-to-date figures.

### Can I deduct both state income tax and sales tax?
No. You must choose to deduct either your state and local income taxes OR your state and local sales taxes. You cannot deduct both. You can, however, add your property taxes to whichever one you choose, up to the total $10,000 limit.

### Do business owners have a workaround for the SALT cap?
Yes. Many states offer a Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) election, which allows business owners to pay state taxes at the entity level, effectively converting them into fully deductible business expenses rather than capped personal itemized deductions.
